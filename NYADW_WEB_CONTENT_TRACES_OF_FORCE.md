## 1. Core Website Goal

The website should introduce New York Asian Design Week as an annual cultural platform, announce the 2026 exhibition theme **Traces of Force**, explain the curatorial direction, promote the open call, and help visitors, designers, artists, partners, sponsors, and press understand and engage with the event.

**Homepage priority: Open Call first.** The open call deadline, requirements, selected-work exhibition venue, and **Submit Work** button should be immediately visible when the homepage loads. Visitors should not need to scroll or navigate to discover how to apply.

The site should feel like a serious cultural platform and exhibition system, not a generic festival page or one-time student show. It should be simple enough to launch quickly, but flexible enough to grow into an annual platform with future editions, archives, event programs, sponsor visibility, artist profiles, and press materials.

The public experience should feel:

- editorial
- spatial
- restrained
- material
- contemporary
- culturally precise
- quietly intense

Avoid generic “Asian cultural festival” styling. The website should position Asian design through material decision, pressure, adaptation, displacement, and contemporary practice — not through motif, ornament, or easily readable cultural symbolism.

## 5. Curatorial Theme Direction

### Public Theme

```text
Traces of Force
```

### Theme Line

```text
Force Forms Matter
```

### Core Idea

Force forms matter.

It bends, compresses, erodes, and resists. Before design becomes an object, image, or space, it passes through conditions that shape it: gravity, pressure, movement, material behavior, bodily habit, environmental change, cultural memory. Some forces can be measured. Others can only be sensed. Once they meet matter, they leave a trace.

**Traces of Force** considers physical design as the record of these encounters. A form is not a finished appearance — it is the consequence of contact. Between idea and material. Body and environment. Memory and transformation.


### Physical / Digital Frame

As the **Physical edition** of New York Asian Design Week’s two-year dialogue between Physical and Digital, the 2026 exhibition turns toward what design becomes when it leaves the screen.

The digital allows form to move with speed, fluidity, and simulation. The physical introduces resistance. It has weight, grain, temperature, and duration. It must be inhabited. The physical does not oppose the digital — it makes different demands of the body.

### Cultural Position

Those demands, in New York, are also cultural.

Asian design is often asked to be visible but easily readable — culturally marked, yet globally fluent. **Traces of Force** refuses that position. It treats Asian design not as a fixed aesthetic, but as a practice under pressure: between restraint and expression, place and displacement, continuity and the force to change.

Identity here is not held in motif. It is formed through material decision, adaptation, and what gets carried forward.

### Closing Thought

A trace is partial and open. It is what remains after contact — not the force itself, but its consequence. Design, seen this way, is not the imposition of form onto matter. It is matter registering what has acted upon it.

## 6. Two-Year Physical / Digital Dialogue

NYADW should be presented as a two-year curatorial dialogue between **Physical** and **Digital** design. The dialogue allows the platform to move between tangible practices and screen-based or networked practices while keeping one continuous question at the center: how contemporary Asian design is shaped, perceived, and carried forward.

The **2026 edition** opens this cycle with a focus on the **Physical**: spaces, objects, materials, environments, installations, products, architecture, craft, and embodied experience. The **2027 edition** will shift toward the **Digital**: interfaces, virtual environments, AI, moving image, media art, online identity, and networked culture.

Use this refined public-facing language where a concise explanation is needed:

> New York Asian Design Week unfolds through a two-year dialogue between Physical and Digital. In 2026, **Traces of Force** begins with the Physical — the weight, grain, pressure, and resistance of matter. In 2027, the conversation will turn toward the Digital, exploring how Asian design is shaped through screens, systems, networks, and virtual space.

| Year | Focus | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 2026 | Physical / Tangible | Spaces, objects, materials, environments, installations, products, architecture, craft, and embodied experience. |
| 2027 | Digital / Virtual | Digital culture, interfaces, virtual environments, AI, moving image, media art, online identity, and networked experience. |
| 2028 | Physical / Tangible | Returns to physical design with a new theme. |
| 2029 | Digital / Virtual | Returns to digital / virtual design with a new theme. |

Use **Physical Edition** as a supporting label, not as the public theme title. The public-facing theme is **Traces of Force**.

## 8. Visual Direction

### Overall Vibe

- Contemporary
- Editorial
- Minimal
- Spatial
- Severe but elegant
- Material and tactile
- Philosophical but accessible
- Cultural but not decorative
- Premium but not corporate

### What to Avoid

Avoid cliché Asian visual references such as:

- Dragons
- Lanterns
- Bamboo
- Red-and-gold festival palette
- Brushstroke calligraphy as decoration
- Generic “Oriental” patterns
- Temple roof silhouettes
- Exoticized heritage imagery

Avoid generic design-festival brightness, startup gradients, or overly playful retro visuals. This identity should feel sharper, quieter, and more exhibition-like.

### Visual Language

Use:

- fine-line contour fields
- pressure-map patterns
- subtle topographic distortions
- dense hairline drawings
- blurred force fields
- graphite grain
- white / off-white surfaces
- black typography
- faint grey lines
- partial fading
- vertical metadata
- unusual margins and asymmetry

The visual system should suggest that form is produced by contact and pressure. It should feel like evidence, not decoration.

| Black | deep black | `#0A0A0A` |

### Color Ratio

- 70–80% off-white / light grey
- 15–20% black or graphite typography
- 5–10% fine-line / pressure-field texture

### Notes

The website should stay primarily monochrome. If an accent color is introduced later, it should be extremely restrained and material in tone, such as mineral olive, oxidized grey-green, rust, or clay. Do not use accent color as the main identity unless the brand direction changes.

## 10. Typography Direction

The type should feel technical, narrow, quiet, and slightly unstable.

### Recommended Type Direction

Use a refined grotesk or condensed sans-serif system.

Suggested premium references:

- Suisse Int’l
- Neue Haas Grotesk
- ABC Diatype
- Favorit
- Akkurat
- Söhne
- Monument Grotesk
- Helvetica Neue Condensed

Suggested free / accessible alternatives:

- Space Grotesk
- Archivo
- Archivo Narrow
- IBM Plex Sans
- IBM Plex Mono
- Roboto Condensed
- Noto Sans

### Recommended Starting Combination

```css
--font-display: "Suisse Int'l", "Neue Haas Grotesk Display", "Archivo Narrow", sans-serif;
--font-body: "Suisse Int'l", "Space Grotesk", "IBM Plex Sans", sans-serif;
--font-meta: "IBM Plex Mono", monospace;
```

### Typographic Style

- Use large, quiet uppercase typography for **TRACES OF FORCE**.
- Use generous letter spacing for the title and metadata.
- Use small uppercase labels for dates, deadlines, venues, and edition information.
- Use vertical type for selected metadata, especially date, venue, and edition label.
- Use narrow, compact CTA buttons.
- Use ghosted duplicate text sparingly as a trace or residue.
- Use short lines and generous margins for curatorial text.

### Avoid

- Fake “Asian-style” fonts
- Brushstroke calligraphy
- Decorative serif fonts
- Overly friendly rounded sans-serif
- Startup-style Inter-only minimalism
- Overly expressive festival display fonts

### Multilingual Readiness

The site should be able to support Chinese text later. Choose font stacks that do not break when Chinese characters are added.

Suggested Chinese fallback:

```css
font-family: var(--font-body), "Noto Sans SC", "Noto Sans TC", "Source Han Sans", sans-serif;
```

For Chinese headings, use a modern Heiti or Songti only if it matches the restrained editorial tone. Avoid decorative calligraphic styles.

## 11. Graphic System

### Main Graphic Device: Force Field / Contour Trace

The central graphic language should use dense, fine-line contour fields as a transformed alternative to the earlier blurred artist reference. The lines should feel like a record of pressure, magnetic force, erosion, topography, or material displacement.

It should not copy any specific artist image. The pattern should be original and systemized.

### Pattern Characteristics

Use:

- dense hairline contour lines
- topographic bends
- vortex-like pressure centers
- subtle optical distortion
- soft graphite grain
- off-white background
- low-contrast grey linework
- areas where lines compress and loosen
- partial fading at the edges

Avoid:

- direct reproduction of artist references
- obvious fingerprints or smoke effects
- decorative waves
- generic topographic map graphics
- overly high-contrast psychedelic op-art
- literal physics diagrams

### Pattern Usage

Use the pattern as:

- hero background
- open call cards
- social media posts
- poster field
- section dividers
- subtle hover texture
- merchandise / tote / badge surface
- exhibition wall graphic

The pattern should always feel quiet and structural. It should not overwhelm the text.

---

## 12. Layout Principles

### 1. Asymmetry

Avoid centered festival-style layouts. Use off-axis composition, rotated text, vertical metadata, unusual margins, and partial crops.

### 2. Large Empty Fields

Whitespace is not passive background. It should feel like a surface under tension.

### 3. Sharp Type / Subtle Surface

The system should contrast:

- crisp, technical typography
- faint contour or pressure-field background

### 4. Partial Visibility

Some patterns or words may be faded, cropped, or ghosted. The viewer should feel that information is being revealed through residue.

### 5. Metadata as Design

Dates, venues, edition labels, and open-call information should be treated as graphic information — small, precise, vertical, and carefully placed.

- hover states that reveal faint linework
- event cards sliding horizontally like records in an archive
- theme keywords appearing along a pressure path
- gentle page transitions

Avoid:

- heavy animations
- fast kinetic typography
- overly playful cursor effects
- decorative parallax
- motion that makes text hard to read

The motion should feel like pressure moving through matter — slow, subtle, and structural.

## 32. Final Creative Direction Summary

NYADW 2026 should feel like a contemporary exhibition platform that reframes Asian design through matter, pressure, resistance, and material consequence. The website should avoid decorative “Oriental” clichés and instead express Asian identity through force, displacement, adaptation, and what gets carried forward.

The visual language should be black and white, off-white, precise, and textural. Use dense fine-line contour fields, subtle pressure patterns, vertical metadata, and quiet asymmetrical layouts. The site should feel like a surface after contact — restrained, physical, and marked by what remains.

The public message should be clear:

```text
Force forms matter.
A form is not a finished appearance.
It is the consequence of contact.
```
